How to install

Two versions, one rule. Apple Mail (Mac & iPhone) uses the Apple Mail buttons on your card. Every other app — Gmail, Outlook, web — uses the Gmail, Outlook & web buttons. The Apple version uses a sharper logo format that Gmail cannot display, which is why the two are separate.

macOS Mail strips HTML pasted into its signature editor, so the reliable method is to create a placeholder signature, then replace the file on disk. Use the Apple Mail group on your card.

  1. In Mail, open Settings → Signatures. Pick your account, click +, type any placeholder text (e.g. "temp"), and give it a name. This creates the file you will edit.
  2. Quit Mail completely with Cmd+Q — not just closing the window.
  3. In Finder press Cmd+Shift+G and paste ~/Library/Mail/V10/MailData/Signatures/. The V10 may differ — open the highest-numbered V folder you have.
  4. Sort by Date Modified. The newest .mailsignature file (its name is a long random ID) is the placeholder you just made.
  5. From the Apple Mail group on your card, download the .mailsignature file. Open it and the placeholder file in a plain-text editor (TextEdit set to plain text, or VS Code).
  6. Copy everything from your downloaded file and paste it over the entire contents of the placeholder file. Save, keeping the .mailsignature extension and the original ID filename.
  7. Select the file in Finder, press Cmd+I, and tick Locked.
  8. Reopen Mail and select your signature for the account.
The Locked step is not optional. Without it, Mail or iCloud sync rewrites the file on next launch and your signature reverts to the placeholder. If that happens, redo steps 5–7 and confirm Locked is checked.

iPhone Mail (the built-in Apple Mail app) pastes HTML cleanly most of the time. Use the Apple Mail group on your card.

  1. On your iPhone, open the Apple Mail group's Open link on your card, in Safari.
  2. Press and hold anywhere on the signature, then tap Select All, then Copy. Use Select All rather than dragging the blue handles — a partial selection is the usual cause of a bad paste.
  3. Go to Settings → Mail → Signature and delete any text already in the box.
  4. Tap in the box and Paste. The logo and formatting should appear.
If the paste comes in wrong — plain text, no logo, or a broken layout — shake the phone and tap Undo in the dialog that pops up (or swipe left with three fingers to undo). Then go back to Safari, do Select All → Copy again, and paste once more. It almost always lands correctly on the second try. The result depends on exactly what the cursor selected, so a clean Select All is the fix.
Using the Gmail app on your phone? That is Gmail, not Apple Mail — follow the Gmail tab and use the Gmail, Outlook & web version instead.

Use the Gmail, Outlook & web group on your card. Do not use the Apple Mail version — its logo will show as a broken image in Gmail.

  1. On your card, click Copy in the Gmail, Outlook & web group (or open that group's Open link, then Select All with Cmd/Ctrl+A and Copy).
  2. In Gmail, go to Settings (gear) → See all settings → General → Signature.
  3. Click Create new, name it, and paste into the editor box.
  4. Under Signature defaults, set it for new emails and for replies/forwards.
  5. Scroll to the bottom and click Save Changes — easy to miss, and nothing saves without it.
If the logo shows as a broken image, you likely pasted the Apple Mail version. Delete it, then copy again from the Gmail, Outlook & web group and re-paste.

Use the Gmail, Outlook & web group on your card. Outlook on the web is the most reliable; the desktop apps can be inconsistent with HTML signatures.

  1. On your card, click Copy in the Gmail, Outlook & web group (or open that group's Open link, Select All and Copy).
  2. Outlook on the web: Settings (gear) → Mail → Compose and reply → Email signature. Paste, name it, set it as default for new messages and replies, and Save.
  3. Outlook desktop: Options → Mail → Signatures (Windows) or Preferences → Signatures (Mac) → New, then paste.
If the desktop app drops the logo or mangles the layout, use Outlook on the web instead — it handles the embedded image reliably.
